Chapter Overview & Clinical Significance

Chapter 1 establishes the foundational connection between microbiology and surgical technology. Understanding microbial science is not optional for the surgical technologist — it is the scientific basis for aseptic technique, sterile field management, and patient safety.

📋 Surgical Conscience

The guiding principle that requires surgical technologists to disclose breaks in sterile technique, whether observed by others or not, and to adhere to best practices in all situations for the benefit of the patient.

  • Cannot see sterility — must trust technique
  • Applies to traditional OR, hybrid rooms, and outpatient centers
🛣 Why Microbiology Matters in the OR
  • Foundation for recognizing sources of infection
  • Basis for aseptic and sterile techniques
  • Supports prevention of surgical site infections (SSIs)
  • Enables effective barrier/PPE use
  • Guides response when chain of infection is identified

Blood-Borne Pathogens & Personal Protective Equipment

Blood-Borne Pathogens (BBP)

Infectious microorganisms found in human blood that can cause disease in humans. Transmission can occur via needlesticks, sharps injuries, or mucous membrane exposure.

OPIMs

Other Potentially Infectious Materials — pathogens transmitted by mechanisms other than blood, such as air, ingestion, or direct contact.

PPE in the OR — PPE provides a two-way barrier: protecting the healthcare worker from patients AND protecting patients from healthcare workers. Components include masks, gowns, gloves, eye shields, hats, shoe covers, lead shields, and laser goggles.

Infection Prevention Responsibilities

  • Transmission cannot always be prevented, but all available measures should be used to break the chain of infection
  • Sterility cannot be seen — adherence to principles of asepsis and sterile technique is critical
  • Broad educational foundation in microbiology enhances skill and proper technique
  • Infection can range from minor annoyance to life-threatening illness

Early Pioneers of Microbiology (Pre-1800s)

These scientists laid the groundwork — developing microscopy, challenging spontaneous generation, and first linking microbes to disease.

1546
Girolamo Fracastoro

First to apply scientific principles to the concept that tiny, unseen organisms spread disease. In his book De Contagione et Contagiais Morbis, he described transmission modes:

  • Direct contact between infected host and others
  • Indirect contact
  • Carried on clothing
  • Through the air
1665
Robert Hooke
  • Designed and built a compound microscope
  • Published Micrographia (1665) — first to use the term "cell"
  • Established cell theory: all living things are composed of cells
  • Later work developed understanding of elasticity
1673–1723
Antonie van Leeuwenhoek — "Father of Microbiology"
  • First to observe and record previously invisible bacteria, protozoa, and other cells from rainwater, saliva, and semen — called them "animalcules"
  • In 1723, submitted the first accurate drawings of bacteria seen with a simple microscope to the Royal Society of London
1668
Francesco Redi — Challenging Abiogenesis

Set out to disprove abiogenesis (life spontaneously originates from nonliving substances).

  • Experiment: decaying meat in sealed vs. open jars
  • Maggots appeared in open jars — none in sealed jars
  • Critics argued fresh oxygen was required for spontaneous generation
  • Follow-up: used fine mesh gauze (allowing oxygen) — same results
  • Beliefs were slow to change despite evidence
1700s
Needham & Spallanzani

Both were priests. Needham attempted to prove a "vital force" for spontaneous generation. Spallanzani attempted to disprove it through scientific experimentation. Their debate advanced scientific method.

Early 1800s
Edward Jenner — "Father of Vaccines"
  • First to effectively provide immunity through vaccination for smallpox
  • Noted milkmaids who contracted cowpox never contracted smallpox
  • Inoculated a boy with cowpox — after recovery, boy showed immunity to smallpox
Mid-1800s
Ignaz Semmelweis — The Handwashing Pioneer

Physician in a maternity hospital who made the critical connection between handwashing and infection prevention:

  • Observed 20–30% maternal death rate from puerperal fever
  • Found that patients seen by medical students (who came from autopsies) had much higher infection rates than those seen by midwives (who washed hands rigorously)
  • Implemented chlorinated solutions for hand hygiene — dropped maternal mortality rate to just over 1%
  • Failed to inspire peers; was institutionalized
  • Died of a surgical site infection in 1865 — tragically ironic

The Golden Age of Microbiology

The mid-to-late 1800s produced the Germ Theory of Disease, aseptic technique in surgery, and the foundation of modern microbiology laboratory science.

Mid-1800s
Louis Pasteur — Germ Theory & Pasteurization

His experiments disproved abiogenesis and vital force theories, leading to major contributions:

  • Discovered the link between yeasts and alcohol — revealed the difference between aerobic and anaerobic bacteria
  • Proved that food spoilage is caused by microorganisms and could be prevented by low-heating — coined pasteurization
  • Discovered a protozoan as the etiology (cause) of a silkworm disease
  • Used a rabies vaccine on a child, risking his professional license
Late 1860s
Joseph Lister — "Father of Modern Surgery"

A surgeon who embraced Pasteur's germ-disease link and transformed surgical practice:

  • Established the first principles of aseptic technique to protect patients, doctors, and personnel
  • Used phenol (carbolic acid) as a skin scrub, pre-op skin prep, on dressings, and as an aerosolized OR spray
  • Insisted on surgical gowns and gloves
  • Required disinfection and boiling of surgical instruments between procedures
OR Connection: Every pre-operative skin prep, every sterile gown and glove donned before surgery, every instrument sterility protocol — these trace directly to Lister's principles.
1876 onward
Robert Koch — Bacteriology & Koch's Postulates

Became famous for discovering rod-shaped bacilli as the cause of anthrax in cattle, then went further:

  • Developed Koch's Postulates — a set of experimental steps linking a specific microorganism to a specific disease (still used today)
  • Developed the pure culture technique using solid agar in Petri dishes and the streak plate method
  • Demonstrated that different species of bacteria cause different types of infections
  • Paved the way for Gram staining methods for classifying microbial species
  • His students included Paul Ehrlich, later known as the "Father of Chemotherapy"

Koch's Postulates

A series of experimental steps used to link a specific microorganism to a specific disease:

1
Presence: The microorganism must be present in all cases of the disease
2
Isolation: It must be isolated from the diseased host and grown in pure culture
3
Inoculation: The cultured microorganism must cause disease when introduced into a healthy host
4
Re-isolation: The microorganism must be re-isolated from the experimentally diseased host and shown to be identical to the original

Note: Exceptions exist related to specific bacteria and diseases (e.g., some pathogens cannot be cultured, asymptomatic carriers).

The Modern Era of Microbiology

The 20th and 21st centuries brought exponential advances in bacteriology, virology, antibiotics, and genetics — along with recognition of previously overlooked contributors.

1933–1936
Ruth Ella Moore
  • First African-American to receive a PhD in bacteriology (1933)
  • First African-American to join the American Society of Microbiology (1936)
  • Led extensive research and eventual treatment of tuberculosis (2nd highest cause of death in the U.S. at the time)
  • Published on immunology, dental caries, and blood type-associated diseases in African-Americans
1944
Elizabeth Bugie — Streptomycin
  • Part of a Rutgers University research team that developed streptomycin — an antibiotic used to treat deadly tuberculosis and other serious infections
  • Her name was removed from the patent by the lead scientist, who felt she would "only get married and have a family"
  • Her male colleagues were awarded the Nobel Prize for Medicine — she was not recognized despite her contributions
1964
June Almeida — First Identification of Coronavirus
  • Scottish-born immigrant to Canada; never completed her undergraduate degree but became a skilled laboratory technician
  • Recognized for techniques for identification of microscopic particles
  • Developed the use of antibodies to identify viruses under magnification
  • First identified the coronavirus in 1964 using the electron microscope — the same type of viral pathogen responsible for the COVID-19 pandemic

A Century of Scientific Breakthroughs

Over the past 120 years, discoveries in microbiology, bacteriology, virology, and genetics have developed exponentially into the 21st century. The microbial world is incredibly resourceful in its survival methods — the human race must find a way to coexist with it. Awareness of disease transmission mechanisms is critical for all healthcare workers, especially those in sterile surgical environments.
Key 20th-Century Terms
  • Antibiotic — substance that kills or inhibits bacterial growth
  • Penicillin — first widely used antibiotic (Fleming, 1928)
  • Quarantine — isolation to prevent disease spread
  • Bioterrorism — deliberate use of pathogens to cause harm
  • Germ warfare — weaponization of biological agents
Lessons for the Surgical Technologist
  • History repeats — COVID-19 echoed past pandemics
  • Patterns exist for outbreak, treatment, eradication, and recurrence
  • Global interconnectedness accelerates disease transmission
  • Science builds on itself — today's SSI prevention protocols stand on centuries of research

Standard Precautions & Infection Control

Standard Precautions are the baseline infection prevention practices applied to ALL patients in ALL healthcare settings, regardless of known infection status. Developed by CDC/HICPAC.

🏙 Key Organizations
  • CDC — Centers for Disease Control and Prevention
  • HICPAC — Healthcare Infection Control Practices Advisory Committee (a special section within the CDC)
  • Baseline document: 2007 Guideline for Isolation Precautions: Preventing Transmission of Infectious Agents in Healthcare Settings (updated 2019)

What Standard Precautions Cover

Standard Precautions combine Universal Precautions (UP) and Body Substance Isolation (BSI). They apply to:

  • All blood and body fluids
  • All secretions and excretions (except sweat)
  • Non-intact skin
  • Mucous membranes

All of the above may contain transmissible infectious agents.

Elements of Standard Precautions

Core Elements
  • Hand hygiene
  • Gloves (when contact with blood/body fluids expected)
  • Gown
  • Mask, eye protection, or face shield (based on anticipated exposure)
  • Safe injection practices
  • Proper handling of contaminated equipment
2019 Additions to Standard Precautions
  • Respiratory Hygiene / Cough Etiquette
  • Safe injection practices (reinforced)
  • Masks for spinal/epidural procedures — catheter insertion or injection into spinal or epidural spaces (myelogram, spinal/epidural anesthesia)

These newer elements focus on protection of patients, not just healthcare workers.

Transmission-Based Precautions

Additional measures taken when the patient's disease or infection status is known. These are layered on top of Standard Precautions:

  • Contact Precautions — direct/indirect contact transmission (MRSA, C. diff)
  • Droplet Precautions — large respiratory droplets (influenza, pertussis)
  • Airborne Precautions — small airborne particles (TB, measles, chickenpox)
HAI Reimbursement Policy: The Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) have revised policies so hospitals must bear the costs of treatment for preventable healthcare-associated infections (HAIs) — including surgical site infections (SSIs). This makes infection prevention directly linked to hospital financial accountability.

OR-Specific Aseptic Techniques (Discussion Activity Topics)

  • Surgical scrub / hand antisepsis prior to gowning and gloving
  • Sterile gowning and closed gloving technique
  • Sterile draping of patient and back table
  • Maintenance of sterile field integrity throughout the procedure
  • Required OR personnel vaccinations (Hepatitis B, influenza, etc.)
  • Correct disposal of sharps and contaminated materials

Key Terms — Chapter 1

Click any card to reveal the definition. These terms reflect the key vocabulary from all four sections of Chapter 1.

Abiogenesis
Click to reveal
The theory (now disproved) that life spontaneously originates from nonliving substances. Challenged by Redi's experiments (1668) and finally refuted by Pasteur.
Aerobic
Click to reveal
Requiring oxygen to live and grow. Aerobic bacteria use oxygen in their metabolic processes. Contrast with anaerobic.
Anaerobic
Click to reveal
Able to live and grow in the absence of oxygen. Some bacteria can cause infection in closed spaces such as abscesses where oxygen is limited.
Antibiotic
Click to reveal
A substance produced by a microorganism or synthesized chemically that kills or inhibits the growth of other microorganisms, particularly bacteria.
Aseptic Technique
Click to reveal
Principles and practices used to prevent contamination of sterile fields, instruments, and tissues. First established by Joseph Lister in the late 1860s. Foundational to surgical technology practice.
Bioterrorism
Click to reveal
The deliberate release or use of biological agents (bacteria, viruses, toxins) to cause harm to people, animals, or the environment for terrorist or warfare purposes.
Blood-Borne Pathogens (BBP)
Click to reveal
Infectious microorganisms found in human blood that can cause diseased conditions in humans. Examples include HIV and Hepatitis B and C viruses. A major occupational hazard for surgical technologists.
Cell Theory
Click to reveal
The fundamental biological principle that all living things are composed of cells. The term "cell" was first used by Robert Hooke in 1665.
Chemotherapy
Click to reveal
In this context, the use of chemical substances to treat disease (especially infection), pioneered by Paul Ehrlich — "Father of Chemotherapy" and student of Robert Koch.
Endemic
Click to reveal
A disease or condition that is consistently present in a particular geographic area or population at a predictable, relatively stable rate.
Epidemic
Click to reveal
A sudden, widespread occurrence of a disease in a community or region, affecting more people than would normally be expected. Greater in scale than endemic but generally confined to one region.
Etiology
Click to reveal
The cause or origin of a disease or condition. For example, Pasteur discovered the etiology of a silkworm disease to be a protozoan organism.
Germ Theory of Disease
Click to reveal
The theory established by Pasteur and Koch stating that specific microorganisms are the cause of specific infectious diseases. This theory revolutionized medicine and is the basis of modern infection control.
Germ Warfare
Click to reveal
The weaponization and deliberate use of biological agents (bacteria, viruses, toxins) as weapons of war or mass destruction against human populations.
Gram Stain
Click to reveal
A laboratory staining technique developed by Hans Gram using Koch's culture methods to classify bacteria into Gram-positive (purple) and Gram-negative (pink) based on cell wall characteristics. Critical for identifying pathogens.
Immunity
Click to reveal
The ability of an organism to resist a specific infection or toxin due to the presence of specific antibodies or sensitized white blood cells. Jenner demonstrated it with smallpox vaccination.
Immunocompromised
Click to reveal
Having a weakened or impaired immune system, making a person more susceptible to infection. Surgical patients may be immunocompromised due to disease, medications, or surgical stress — requiring heightened infection prevention.
Inoculated
Click to reveal
Introduced a pathogen or vaccine into the body to produce immunity or study the organism's effects. Jenner inoculated a child with cowpox to confer smallpox immunity.
Koch's Postulates
Click to reveal
A set of four experimental criteria developed by Robert Koch to establish that a specific microorganism causes a specific disease. Still used in microbiology labs today, with recognized exceptions.
OPIMs
Click to reveal
Other Potentially Infectious Materials — pathogens transmitted by mechanisms other than blood, such as air, ingestion, or direct contact. Includes body fluids, mucous membranes, and non-intact skin.
Pandemic
Click to reveal
An epidemic that spreads across multiple countries or continents, affecting a large number of people worldwide. COVID-19 (2020) is the most recent example.
Pasteurization
Click to reveal
The process of low-heating food, milk, or beverages to destroy harmful bacteria without significantly changing flavor. Developed by Louis Pasteur after discovering the link between microorganisms and food spoilage.
Penicillin
Click to reveal
The first widely used antibiotic, discovered by Alexander Fleming in 1928. It opened the era of antibiotic medicine and transformed treatment of bacterial infections globally.
Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)
Click to reveal
Equipment worn to create a barrier against infection transmission. In the OR, PPE includes masks, gowns, gloves, eye shields, hats, shoe covers, and specialized items like lead shields or laser goggles. Provides two-way protection.
Pure Culture Technique
Click to reveal
A laboratory method developed by Robert Koch to isolate and grow a single species of microorganism using solid agar in Petri dishes and the streak plate method. Essential for identifying specific pathogens.
Puerperal Fever
Click to reveal
Also called "childbed fever" — a serious bacterial infection of the reproductive tract following childbirth. Semmelweis linked it to unwashed hands of physicians and reduced deaths from ~25% to ~1% with chlorinated handwashing.
Quarantine
Click to reveal
The isolation of people, animals, or goods that have been exposed to a contagious disease, to prevent the potential spread of infection to others.
Sterilant
Click to reveal
A chemical or physical agent capable of destroying all forms of microbial life, including bacterial spores. Sterilants are used to process surgical instruments and are distinct from disinfectants.
Surgical Conscience
Click to reveal
The ethical guiding principle requiring surgical technologists to disclose all breaks in sterile technique — whether observed by others or not — and to adhere consistently to best practices for the patient's benefit.
Vaccination
Click to reveal
The process of administering a vaccine to stimulate the immune system to develop protection against a specific disease. First demonstrated effectively by Edward Jenner with smallpox in the early 1800s.

1. Which scientist is known as the "Father of Microbiology" for first observing and recording bacteria and protozoa?

✓ Correct! Van Leeuwenhoek first observed bacteria, protozoa, and other cells (calling them "animalcules") and submitted accurate drawings to the Royal Society of London in 1723.
✗ Incorrect. Antonie van Leeuwenhoek is the "Father of Microbiology" — he was the first to observe and record bacteria using a simple microscope.

2. Ignaz Semmelweis reduced maternal mortality from puerperal fever to approximately 1% by implementing which measure?

✓ Correct! Semmelweis discovered that physicians coming directly from autopsies were spreading infection. Chlorinated handwashing dropped maternal mortality from ~25% to just over 1%.
✗ Incorrect. Semmelweis implemented chlorinated solution handwashing for physicians and students between patient contacts, dramatically reducing puerperal fever deaths.

3. Joseph Lister is known as the "Father of Modern Surgery" primarily because he:

✓ Correct! Lister applied Pasteur's Germ Theory to surgery, establishing aseptic technique using phenol (carbolic acid) for skin prep, dressings, and OR sprays, plus requiring gowns, gloves, and instrument sterilization.
✗ Incorrect. Lister established the first principles of aseptic technique and used carbolic acid (phenol) in the OR to prevent surgical infections.

4. Which of the following best describes "surgical conscience"?

✓ Correct! Surgical conscience is the guiding ethical principle that requires ST's to self-report breaks in sterile technique for the benefit of the patient — regardless of whether anyone else observed the break.
✗ Incorrect. Surgical conscience is the principle requiring a surgical technologist to disclose all breaks in sterile technique, whether observed by others or not, for the benefit of the patient.

5. Koch's Postulates were developed to accomplish which of the following?

✓ Correct! Koch's Postulates provide a set of criteria to experimentally establish that a specific microorganism is the causative agent of a specific disease — still used in microbiology labs today.
✗ Incorrect. Koch's Postulates are experimental criteria used to definitively link a specific microorganism to a specific disease.

6. Standard Precautions apply to which patients in healthcare settings?

✓ Correct! Standard Precautions apply to ALL patients in ALL healthcare settings, regardless of known infection status — treating all blood, body fluids, non-intact skin, and mucous membranes as potentially infectious.
✗ Incorrect. Standard Precautions apply to ALL patients regardless of their suspected or confirmed infection status — the foundational principle of universal protection.

7. June Almeida is historically significant because she was the first to:

✓ Correct! June Almeida, a largely self-taught laboratory technician, first identified the coronavirus in 1964 — the same type of viral pathogen responsible for COVID-19.
✗ Incorrect. June Almeida was the first person to identify the coronavirus, which she did in 1964 using the electron microscope and her technique of using antibodies to visualize viruses.

8. PPE in the surgical setting is described as providing protection in which direction(s)?

✓ Correct! PPE provides a two-way barrier — protecting healthcare workers from patients AND protecting patients from healthcare workers. This bidirectional protection is central to Standard Precautions.
✗ Incorrect. PPE in the OR provides a two-way barrier — protecting both the healthcare worker from patients and patients from healthcare workers.

9. Francesco Redi's experiments with decaying meat in sealed vs. open jars were designed to challenge which theory?

✓ Correct! Redi designed his 1668 experiment to disprove abiogenesis — the belief that life spontaneously originates from nonliving matter. His experiments showed maggots only appeared on meat exposed to the air (and flies), not in sealed containers.
✗ Incorrect. Redi's experiments challenged abiogenesis — the widely held belief that life could spontaneously arise from nonliving substances like decaying meat.

10. The 2019 update to CDC Standard Precautions added which new requirement specifically for patient protection during lumbar puncture or epidural procedures?

✓ Correct! The 2019 updated Standard Precautions added the requirement for masks during insertion of catheters or injection of material into spinal or epidural spaces (myelograms, spinal/epidural anesthesia) — focused on patient protection.
✗ Incorrect. The 2019 update added the requirement to wear masks during insertion of catheters or injection into spinal/epidural spaces — a patient-focused addition to Standard Precautions.
